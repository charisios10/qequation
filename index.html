Quadratic Equations Guide

An open-source guide to understanding and solving quadratic equations with ease!

What is a Quadratic Equation?

A quadratic equation is a polynomial equation of the second degree, written in the standard form as $$ax^2 + bx + c = 0$$ where \(a \), \(b \), and \(c \) are constants and \(x \) is the variable.

Methods to Solve Quadratic Equations

Using the Quadratic Formula

The quadratic formula is a powerful tool for solving any quadratic equation. It is given by:

$$x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{Δ}}{2a}$$

Where \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) are the coefficients from the standard form of the quadratic equation.

The discriminant \(Δ = b^2 - 4ac\) determines the nature of the roots:

Condition Nature of Roots
If \(Δ > 0\) Two distinct real roots
If \(Δ = 0\) Exactly one real root (a repeated root)
If \(Δ < 0\) No real roots (the roots are complex)

Finding the discriminant \(Δ = b^2 - 4ac\) is crucial for determining the nature of the roots of a quadratic equation.

After finding the discriminant, substitute it into the quadratic formula to solve for the roots.

Depending on the value of the discriminant, the quadratic equation may have two distinct real roots, one repeated real root, or no real roots (complex roots).

Solving for roots has 2 types of formulas depending on whether the discriminant is \(Δ > 0\) or \(Δ = 0\) else \(Δ < 0\) is not possible for \(\mathbb{R}\).

Formulas for Roots Based on Discriminant

Condition Formula for Roots
If \(Δ > 0\) $$x_1 = \frac{-b + \sqrt{Δ}}{2a}, \quad x_2 = \frac{-b - \sqrt{Δ}}{2a}$$
If \(Δ = 0\) $$x = \frac{-b}{2a}$$
If \(Δ < 0\) No real roots for \(\mathbb{R}\)

Examples

Example 1: One Real Root

Consider the quadratic equation \(2x^2 + 4x + 2 = 0\).

Here, \(a = 2\), \(b = 4\), and \(c = 2\).

The discriminant is:

$$Δ = b^2 - 4ac = 16 - 16 = 0$$

Since the discriminant is zero, there is exactly one real root:

$$x = \frac{-b}{2a} = \frac{-4}{4} = -1$$

Visualization

Example 2: Two Distinct Real Roots

Consider the quadratic equation \(x^2 - 5x + 6 = 0\).

Here, \(a = 1\), \(b = -5\), and \(c = 6\).

The discriminant is:

$$Δ = b^2 - 4ac = 25 - 24 = 1$$

Since the discriminant is positive, there are two distinct real roots:

$$x_1 = \frac{5 + \sqrt{1}}{2} = \frac{5 + 1}{2} = 3$$ $$x_2 = \frac{5 - \sqrt{1}}{2} = \frac{5 - 1}{2} = 2$$

Example 3: No Real Roots

Consider the quadratic equation \(x^2 + 1 = 0\).

Here, \(a = 1\), \(b = 0\), and \(c = 1\).

The discriminant is:

$$Δ = b^2 - 4ac = 0 - 4 = -4$$

Since the discriminant is negative, there are no real roots (the roots are complex):

No real roots exist for \(\mathbb{R}\) in the equation \(x^2 + 1 = 0\).

Conclusion

Remember that the quadratic formula can be used to solve any quadratic equation, regardless of whether the coefficients are real or complex. However, the nature of the roots will depend on the discriminant as explained above.
